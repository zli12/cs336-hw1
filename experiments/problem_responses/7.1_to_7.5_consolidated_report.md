# 7.1–7.5 Consolidated Report (TinyStories + OWT + Leaderboard)

A single-document tour of the §7 experimental program, from logging
infrastructure (§7.1) through the OpenWebText leaderboard speedrun (§7.5).
Each section is a stand-alone summary of an existing detailed report — the
aim here is to collect the headline numbers, the *learnings* that compounded
from one phase to the next, and the cross-cutting themes in one place.
Pointers to the per-problem deep dives are given in §"Cross-references".

All §7.1–§7.4 runs were on a single NVIDIA A10G (~22 GB). §7.5 is on a
single NVIDIA A100-40GB SXM, with the leaderboard envelope computed by an
explicit A100 → H100 conversion (see §"§7.5 Leaderboard speedrun" below).

---

## Executive summary

Three final models came out of §7.1–§7.5. All share the decoder-only
Transformer skeleton — RoPE, RMSNorm pre-norm, SwiGLU FFN (kept across all
phases, including a §7.5 ReLU² ablation that regressed), AdamW + grad-clip
1.0:

| Final model | Data | Arch | Vocab | Schedule | Best val | Best PPL | Wall (s) | Tokens | Target met? |
|---|---|---|---:|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| `lr-final-cosine-lr2e-3` | TinyStories | 4-layer 512-d | 10,000 | cosine 10K, warmup=500, lr 2e-3 → 2e-4 | **1.3489** @ 9,600 | 3.85 | 5,237 | 327 M | **Yes** (≤ 1.45, margin −0.10 nats) |
| `owt-final-cosine-bs96-lr2.45e-3` | OpenWebText | 4-layer 512-d | 32,000 | cosine 10K, warmup=500, lr 2.45e-3 → 2.45e-4 | **4.0167** @ 10,000 | 55.55 | 8,125 | 245 M | **Yes** (≤ 5.0, margin −0.98 nats) |
| `leaderboard-final` | OpenWebText | **8-layer 1024-d** | 32,000 | **WSD 45K, warmup=100, decay_frac=0.2, lr 1.414e-3 → 0** | **3.1904** @ 45,000 | **24.30** | 10,584 | **1.106 B** | **Yes** (1.354 h H100-equivalent at 2.17× midpoint, inside the 1.5 h envelope) |

The single recipe family that produced all three was *built up empirically*
through:

- §7.2 LR sweep → `lr* ≈ 2e-3` for `bs=128`,
- §7.2 batch sweep → sqrt-LR scaling rule validates across `bs ∈ [1, 192]`,
- §7.2 cosine A/B → +0.10 nats at the same 10K-step budget on TinyStories
  (decay does the work, warmup is a small cost),
- §7.3 four ablations → RMSNorm, pre-norm, RoPE, SwiGLU each contribute,
- §7.4 LR/bs re-probe on OWT → `lr=2.45e-3, bs=96` (constraint: bs=128 OOMs at
  `vocab=32k`),
- §7.5 phased speedrun → infra (bf16 + SDPA + compile, 7.32× throughput),
  Tier-1 quality stack (QK-Norm + tied embeddings + soft-cap + z-loss),
  scale to `(d=1024, L=8)`, Muon-mixed optimizer, WSD schedule, ctx=1024.

**Decay-driven gains compounded the most**, in every phase that had one:

| Phase | Decay-tail gain | Source |
|---|---:|---|
| §7.2 cosine 10K (TinyStories) | **−0.10** nats | warmup = small early tax; decay = entire win |
| §7.4 cosine 10K (OWT) | **−0.44** nats | last 7K decay steps moved val 4.458 → 4.017 |
| §7.5 WSD 45K (OWT leaderboard) | **−0.21** nats | last 9K decay steps moved val 3.397 → 3.190 |

The §7.5 final number (val `3.1904`, PPL `24.3`) sits in the same
neighborhood as published small-LM numbers (e.g. GPT-2 small at val ~3.0
with ~10× more training data), at a scale we trained from scratch in under
3 hours.

## §7.1 Logging infrastructure

`scripts/train_lm.py` writes metrics to three places, chosen so loss curves
can be plotted against both **gradient steps** and **wallclock seconds**
without re-running a job:

- **Console**: `step`, `train_loss` / `val_loss`, `tokens_per_sec`, `elapsed_s`.
- **CSV** (`--metrics-csv <path>`): one row per logging event with columns
  `step,wallclock_sec,split,loss,tokens_per_sec,lr`. This is what every
  ablation table in §7.2–§7.5 reads.
- **Weights & Biases** (optional): `train/loss`, `val/loss`,
  `perf/tokens_per_sec`, `time/elapsed_sec`, all aligned to `step`.

Why both step and wallclock: §7.2's batch-size sweep makes the divergence
visible — at equal optimizer steps `bs=192` wins (val 1.520) but at equal
*seconds* `bs=64` is miles ahead for the first ~600 s. §7.5's phase-by-phase
ablations rely on the wallclock axis to make decisions ("is the −0.06-nat
ctx=1024 win worth the per-step time?"). Either axis alone would tell a
misleading story; storing both lets us choose post-hoc.

Plotting helper: `scripts/plot_runs.py` consumes the CSV and produces the
SVGs cited throughout the rest of the report.

### §7.2 Learning-rate sweep, edge-of-stability, and the cosine win

brackets the previously strong baseline regime and probes the upper edge:

| `lr` | Best val (3K) |
|---:|---:|
| 3e-4 | 1.717 (under-fit) |
| 1e-3 | 1.596 |
| **2e-3** | **1.585** ← winner |

Curve: `7.2_learning_rate_sweep.svg`. The optimum is broad — `2e-3` and

**Edge-of-stability probe (1000 steps each, fixed schedule, `bs=128`).**
Multipliers `5×` to `500×` of `lr*=2e-3`:

| `lr` | Best val | Final val | Behavior |
|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 1e-2 | 2.815 | 2.815 | stable, much worse |
| 2e-2 | 3.058 | 3.058 | stable, much worse |
| 4e-2 | 3.602 | 3.622 | stable, poor |
| 8e-2 | 4.308 | 4.475 | stable, very poor |
| 2e-1 | 4.619 | 5.468 | **erratic, drifting up** |
| 1e0  | 21.83 | 89.85 | **divergent / NaN-territory** |

Curve: `7.2_edge_of_stability.svg`. Clean monotonic transition: from
`1e-2` to `8e-2` the model trains, just badly; at `2e-1` the trajectory turns

`50–100 × lr*`.

**Cosine vs fixed (10K steps each, the deliverable run).** The fixed-LR 10K
baseline missed `≤ 1.45` by `+0.004`. A cosine warmup-and-decay schedule at
the same compute budget closed the gap and gave `−0.10` nats of margin:

| Run | bs | lr_max | lr_min | warmup | Best val | Step @ Best | Wall (s) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `lr-final-tuned-fixed-lr2e-3` | 128 | 2e-3 | — | 0 | 1.4536 | 8,600 | 5,277 |
| `lr-final-cosine-lr2e-3` | 128 | 2e-3 | 2e-4 | 500 | **1.3489** | 9,600 | 5,237 |

Curve: `7.2_lr_final_cosine_vs_fixed.svg`. Cosine cycle = 10K steps.

**The decomposition that mattered.** Per-step gap (cosine − fixed):

| step | cosine − fixed |
|---:|---:|
| 200 | **+0.27** (warmup tax) |
| 1000 | +0.04 |
| 2000 | +0.003 (parity) |
| 3000 | **−0.011** (cosine first wins) |
| 8000 | −0.10 |
| 10000 | **−0.110** (decay-driven) |

Warmup *cost* us 0.27 nats early; cosine doesn't catch up until step ~2000;
the entire `−0.10` end-of-run win comes from the **decay phase**. This is the
recurring lesson: decay finds a sharper minimum, warmup is a (small) cost
worth paying only if peak LR is near the edge of stability. **§7.5 picks
this up directly** — WSD makes the decay window an explicit
`decay_frac` parameter instead of an integral of a cosine curve, and at 45K
steps the WSD decay tail alone contributed `−0.207` nats.

### §7.2 Batch-size sweep (3000 steps, sqrt-LR scaled from `bs=128, lr=2e-3`)

LR scaling rule: `lr(bs) = 2e-3 × sqrt(bs / 128)`.

| `bs` | `lr` | Best val | tok/s | Wall (s) | Tokens seen |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 2e-4 | 3.077 | 10.6 k | 76 | 0.77 M |
| 8 | 5e-4 | 2.303 | 52.3 k | 121 | 6.14 M |
| 32 | 1e-3 | 1.864 | 61.0 k | 415 | 24.6 M |
| 64 | 1.4e-3 | 1.713 | 63.2 k | 802 | 49.2 M |
| 128 (anchor) | 2e-3 | 1.585 | 64.4 k | 1,573 | 98.3 M |
| **192** | 2.5e-3 | **1.520** | 56.2 k | 2,697 | 147.5 M |

Plus two LR controls at the anchor LR:

| Control | Best val | Δ vs sqrt-scaled |
|---|---:|---:|
| `bs=8, lr=2e-3` | 2.487 | **+0.18** (anchor LR too aggressive at small bs) |
| `bs=192, lr=2e-3` | 1.523 | +0.003 (≈ tied at large bs) |

Plots: `7.2_batch_size_sweep.svg`, `7.2_batch_size_lr_controls.svg`.

**Findings:**

1. **At equal optimizer steps, larger bs wins monotonically** — every step
   processes more tokens and gradient noise drops. Diminishing returns kick
   in past `bs=128`.
2. **At equal wallclock, smaller batches dominate early.** `bs=32` reaches
   val ~2.0 around 100 s while `bs=128` is still at val ~3.0; large batches
   only overtake past ~600 s.
3. **GPU throughput plateaus by `bs ≈ 32`** (~52 k tok/s at bs=8, ~63 k tok/s
   at bs=64–128). `bs=192` actually drops to 56 k due to allocator/cache
   pressure, but you spend the remaining VRAM well: `bs=192` reaches the
   lowest val at the same step count.
4. **Sqrt-LR rule is essential at small batches, neutral at large.**
   `bs=8` paid `+0.18` nats with the anchor LR; `bs=192` was within
   `0.003`. **§7.5 reuses the rule** for both bs and `d_model` scaling
   (μP-style `lr / sqrt(d_new / d_old)` as a *seed* for a re-tuning sweep).
5. **Memory ceiling at `bs=192`** with `PYTORCH_CUDA_ALLOC_CONF=expandable_segments:True`.

### §7.2 Generation (TinyStories cosine checkpoint)

Sampler: `scripts/generate_lm.py` (temperature + top-p nucleus). Five samples
under `experiments/generations/cosine/`. The headline 325-token continuation
(`temp=0.8, top_p=0.95`, EOS-disabled) shows complete short stories with
clean grammar, named entities tracked across paragraphs, dialogue with
quoted speech, and natural `<|endoftext|>` termination — i.e. the model
learned document boundaries.

The cosine checkpoint (val `1.349`) is visibly better than the fixed-LR
checkpoint (val `1.475`): tighter entity tracking, more natural dialogue,
fewer "stuck on a phrase" loops. ~10% perplexity reduction translates
directly into a step-up in coherence.

Factors observed to affect generation quality:

1. Total training compute / total tokens seen.
2. Decoding hyperparams: `temp=0.8, top_p=0.95` was the sweet spot;
   `temp=0.7` was over-deterministic, `temp=1.0` introduced odd subplots.
3. LR schedule shape (warmup + decay).
4. Tokenizer / vocab quality.
5. Model capacity (depth, width, heads).
6. Architecture choices (RMSNorm, RoPE, SwiGLU) — quantified in §7.3.

## §7.3 Architecture ablations (3000 steps each, `bs=128, lr=2e-3, fixed`)

All four ablations are A/B'd against the reused
`lr-sweep-bs128-fixed-lr2e-3` baseline (val 1.585) with one architectural
flag changed.

| Ablation | Flag | Outcome at `lr=2e-3` | Recovery / Δ vs baseline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Remove RMSNorm | `--no-rms-norm` | **NaN by step ~550** (loss explodes 5+ orders of magnitude) | Stable at `lr=5e-4`: val **1.684** (`+0.10` nats) |
| Post-norm | `--post-norm` | Stuck at val ≈ **5.81** (≈ unigram entropy) | No recovery in 3000 steps; needs warmup + lower LR |
| No RoPE | `--no-rope` | Stable | Val **1.667** (`+0.08` nats; gap opens early, stays constant) |
| SwiGLU → SiLU (param-matched, `d_ff=2048`) | `--ffn-type silu --d-ff 2048` | Stable | Val **1.586** (tied at convergence; SwiGLU's `+0.21`-nat lead at step 200 vanishes by step ~2000) |

Plots: `7.3_layer_norm_ablation.svg`, `7.3_layer_norm_stable.svg`,
`7.3_pre_norm_ablation.svg`, `7.3_no_pos_emb.svg`, `7.3_swiglu_ablation.svg`.

### What each ablation actually taught

**RMSNorm (`layer_norm_ablation`).** Without RMSNorm, residual contributions
stack across blocks with no rescaling — RMS grows roughly linearly with
depth and SwiGLU amplifies it bilinearly, pushing activations into FP-overflow
in a few hundred steps. RMSNorm has *two* effects: (1) **forward-pass scale
control** (normalizes block input to RMS=1), and (2) **effective-LR scaling**
(without it, each sublayer's effective LR is amplified by the growing input
norm). Recovering at `lr=5e-4` traded `+0.10` nats for stability — i.e. RMSNorm
isn't just stability, it lets you *use* the higher LR that produces the
best result.

**Pre-norm vs post-norm (`pre_norm_ablation`).** Post-norm at the same
`lr=2e-3` failed to learn — train loss dropped 9.3 → 5.7 in the first 600
steps, then plateaued at unigram entropy for the remaining 2400 steps. Cause:
post-norm renormalizes after the residual addition, so gradients from later
layers must propagate back through *every* RMSNorm. The Jacobian's
`1/RMS(input)` factor compounds over depth → vanishing gradients in early
layers. Pre-norm gives gradients an identity path through the residual stream
regardless of depth. This is the canonical reason modern Transformers are
pre-norm; we reproduced it cleanly at `(d=512, L=4, lr=2e-3)`.

**RoPE (`no_pos_emb`).** NoPE trains stably and tracks RoPE's curve shape,
just `+0.07–0.08` nats above it for the entire 3000-step run. The gap opens
within the first 200 steps and stays roughly constant — NoPE is paying a
*fixed efficiency cost* across training, not failing to converge. Causal
masking gives some position info for free (a position-`i` token can only
attend to `0..i`), so the model can infer position from receptive-field
structure alone, but it has to re-learn what RoPE provides analytically. We
expect the gap to *grow* at longer contexts; at our `ctx=256` it's a modest
constant tax. (§7.5 went to `ctx=1024` and kept RoPE.)

**SwiGLU vs SiLU (`swiglu_ablation`).** At parameter parity (`d_ff=2048` for
SiLU vs `d_ff=1344` for SwiGLU; total params within 0.6%), the two are
**tied at convergence** (val 1.585 vs 1.586). The interesting story is the
trajectory: SwiGLU has a `+0.21`-nat lead at step 200, `+0.05` at step 600,
and the gap fully collapses by step ~2000. **SwiGLU's gating acts as an
optimizer warmup, not as added expressive capacity at this scale.** §7.5
revisited this: ReLU² FFN (the speedrun's preferred activation) regressed by
+0.020 nats vs SwiGLU at `(d=1024, L=8)` and was dropped — confirming SwiGLU
keeps its small early-training edge even at scale.

### What the four ablations tell us *together*

The §7.4 OWT and §7.5 leaderboard recipes both inherit all four choices
(pre-norm RMSNorm, RoPE, SwiGLU). The above ablations explain why we don't
revisit them on OWT:

- Removing RMSNorm or pre-norm is catastrophic at our chosen LR; recovering
  costs nats.
- Removing RoPE costs `+0.08` nats at the small TinyStories context and
  would likely cost more at a larger one — keeping it is essentially free.
- Swapping SwiGLU → SiLU (or → ReLU²) is neutral-to-negative; we keep
  SwiGLU because it speeds early convergence.

## §7.4 OWT main experiment

Re-run the same architecture and same total iteration count (10K steps) on
**OpenWebText** with `vocab_size=32000` and a freshly-cleaned
`owt-train.uint16.npy` (2.73 B tokens; an earlier tokenization had left 75%
of the array as zero-padding).

### LR re-probe (`bs=64`, fixed schedule, 3000 steps each)

| `lr` | Best val (3K) |
|---:|---:|
| 1e-3 | 4.626 (still trending) |
| **2e-3** | **4.600** ← winner |
| 3e-3 | 4.624 |

Optimum is shallow; **`lr=2e-3` transfers cleanly from TinyStories** to OWT
even at `bs=64`. Encouraging — the LR landscape doesn't move much with the
3.2× larger vocab.

### Batch-size probe (sqrt-LR scaled, 3000 steps each)

A10G memory ceiling matters more on OWT: at `vocab=32k`, the logits tensor
+ grad is `bs × ctx × vocab × 4 B` ≈ 4 GiB at `bs=128` (vs ~1.3 GiB on
TinyStories at the same bs). So:

| `bs` | `lr` (sqrt-scaled) | Best val (3K) | Wall (s) |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 128 | 2.83e-3 | — | **OOM** at step 1 |
| **96** | **2.45e-3** | **4.458** | 2,417 |

`bs=96` beat `bs=64` by `−0.142 nats` at step 3000 (4.458 vs 4.600), at
~2× the wallclock per step. Since the deliverable specifies *same iteration
count* as TinyStories, we optimize for val@10K and pick `bs=96`. (For
wallclock-efficiency `bs=64` is actually a better choice — reaches val 4.6
in ~1200 s vs `bs=96`'s ~2400 s.)

### Final run

| Run | bs | lr_max | lr_min | warmup | sched | Steps | Best val | PPL | Wall (s) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `owt-final-cosine-bs96-lr2.45e-3` | 96 | 2.45e-3 | 2.45e-4 | 500 | cosine | 10,000 | **4.0167** | 55.55 | 8,125 |

Cosine warmup=500, lr_min = lr_max/10, decay over the full 10K cycle. Plots:
`7.4_owt_learning_curve.svg`, `7.4_owt_vs_tinystories.svg`.

**Decay does the work, again.** Val moved from `5.25` (end of warmup) →
`4.458` (step 3000, end of bs probe) → **`4.017`** (step 10000) — the last
**`−0.441 nats`** all came from the cosine decay phase as LR dropped from
`2.09e-3` to `2.45e-4`. This mirrors the TinyStories finding *without
re-running a fixed-vs-cosine A/B on OWT*; we relied on the recipe transferring
and it did.

### TinyStories vs OWT, side by side

| Model | Vocab | Best val | Best PPL | Wall (s) | Tokens |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| TinyStories cosine 10K | 10,000 | 1.3489 | 3.85 | 5,237 | 327 M |
| OWT cosine 10K | 32,000 | 4.0167 | 55.55 | 8,125 | 245 M |
| **Δ (OWT − TS)** | 3.2× | **+2.668** | **+51.7** | +2,888 | −82 M |

The +2.668-nat gap is **a property of the data, not of training**:

1. **Vocabulary 3.2× larger.** `log(32000) − log(10000) ≈ 1.16` nats of
   unavoidable headroom for a uniform model; real models sit somewhere
   below that ceiling but the directional contribution is clear.
2. **Distribution complexity.** TinyStories is curated children's stories
   (small entity set, templated narrative structure); OWT spans news, forum
   posts, recipes, code, marketing copy, fiction. A 22 M-non-embedding-param
   model can near-memorize the former and only sketch the latter.
3. **Effective coverage.** TS at `bs=128` × 10K = 327 M tokens is ~60% of one
   epoch of its 540 M-token train set. OWT at `bs=96` × 10K = 245 M tokens
   is ~9% of one epoch on the 2.73 B-token set. The OWT model effectively
   never re-sees a batch.

§7.5 closed the gap from the *capacity / compute / coverage* side: scale
the model 6×, get 7× more throughput from infra, and use 4.5× more tokens
under the same 1.5 h H100 budget.

### OWT generation samples

Five samples in `experiments/generations/owt/` (mirroring the TinyStories
layout). What we observe lines up exactly with the loss numbers:

- **Local lexical fluency holds** (spelling, morphology, punctuation, quotes
  all clean).
- **Genre / register is plausible per-paragraph** — a press-style prompt
  produces press-style continuations with hedge phrases and named-entity
  scaffolding.
- **Global coherence is weak** — the headline `"Once upon a time"` prompt
  collapses into sports-column / training-camp prose with invented names
  within two sentences.
- **Topic drift inside paragraphs.** Locally well-formed sentences that
  don't form one topic.
- **Lower temperature → degenerate repetition** (`temp=0.7` loops on
  `"the internet, the internet, the internet, ..."`).

Same model + same compute budget produces a *much* more fluent generation on
TinyStories than on OWT — exactly because TinyStories has a 14× smaller
effective branching factor at validation (PPL 3.85 vs 55.6) and OWT requires
spending capacity across many domains that the small model can't fully fit.

## §7.5 Leaderboard speedrun (OpenWebText, 1.5 h H100 envelope)

Open-ended speedrun: starting from the §7.4 baseline (4-layer / 512-d, val
4.02 in 2.26 h on A10G), train the best OWT model under a fixed
**1.5 h H100 bf16-dense** compute envelope. Available hardware was a single
A100-40GB SXM, so we used the canonical **2.17× A100→H100 bf16-dense
ratio** (range 2.0–2.5×) as the conversion factor: `1.5 h H100 ≈ 11,700 s
on A100-40GB`.

### Headline result

- **Final val: `3.1904`** at step 45,000 (perplexity **24.30**).
- Wallclock: **10,584 s = 2 h 56 min** on A100-40GB →
  `1.354 h H100-equivalent` at 2.17× midpoint
  (range `1.176 h – 1.470 h` across the 2.0–2.5× ratio band; *all three
  points fit inside the 1.5 h envelope*).
- Tokens trained: **1.106 B** (45,000 × 24,576 tok/step).
- **−0.826 nats / 2.29× lower PPL** than the §7.4 baseline at the matched
  envelope.

| Run | Val loss | PPL | Tokens | Wallclock | Hardware |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| §7.4 OWT main_experiment | 4.0167 | 55.55 | 245 M | 8,125 s | A10G |
| **§7.5 leaderboard final** | **3.1904** | **24.30** | **1.106 B** | **10,584 s** | **A100-40GB** |
| **Δ vs §7.4** | **−0.826 nats** | **−2.29×** | **+4.5×** | +1.30× | A10G→A100 |

| Knob | Value | Phase the choice came from |
|---|---|---|
| Architecture | `d=1024, L=8, num_heads=16, d_ff=2752, ctx=1024, vocab=32000, rope_theta=10000` | Phase 2 size sweep + Phase 4 ctx swap |

### Phase walkthrough — what was tried, what stuck

The plan ([`7.5_leaderboard_plan.md`](./7.5_leaderboard_plan.md)) interleaved
*cheap LR re-anchors* with implementation phases, so every time the loss
landscape changed we re-tuned LR before continuing. Each row below applies
*cumulatively* on top of the previous row (Phase 0.5 baseline = same arch as

**What was dropped along the way:**

- **ReLU² FFN** (Phase 4b): val@2000 → 4.0149 (`+0.020` nats vs Phase 4a).
  Faster per-step but worse loss at our scale — the speedrun-mod transfer
  caveat landing exactly here.
- **Value-embedding skip @ L4** (Phase 4c): essentially neutral within ±0.005
  nats. Not worth the code complexity at `d=1024, L=8`.
- **Standalone `embed_init_std=1/√d` without weight tying** (Phase 1): regressed
  by +0.041 nats. The 1/√d init is auto-applied when tying; using it
  standalone leaves the LM head over-scaled.

### The three modification tiers (what & why)

The full mechanism + paper-citation discussion lives in
[`7.5_leaderboard_report.md`](./7.5_leaderboard_report.md) §"Modifications
introduced in this section". One-liner per tier here:

- **Tier 0 (infra, ≈ free).** bf16 autocast + SDPA / FA2 + `torch.compile`
  + TF32 + cudnn.benchmark.  Combined effect at the §7.4 shape: 220 K tok/s
  on A100 vs 30 K tok/s on the §7.4 A10G fp32 setup — **7.32×** throughput
  uplift, val-loss drift ≤ +0.025 nats over the 200-step probe.
- **Tier 1 (quality wins, kept by default).** QK-Norm (per-head RMSNorm of
  Q and K post-RoPE, bounds dot products), weight tying with reduced embed
  init `std = 1/√d_model` (saves ~16 M params, frees memory for a bigger
  model), Gemma-2 logit soft-cap (`cap·tanh(logits/cap)`, `cap=30`), PaLM
  z-loss (`1e-4 · mean(logsumexp(logits)²)`). All four are KEEP; sum of
  singles was −0.065 nats but stacked the same four mods got **−0.082** —
  super-linear stacking.
- **Tier 2 (speedrun-style, ablate).** Muon optimizer on hidden 2D weights
  (Newton-Schulz orthogonalization of momentum buffer; **kept**, biggest
  single win at −0.192 nats), WSD schedule (kept, `−0.121` at 2K steps and
  `−0.207` at 45K steps), ctx=1024 with bs=24 (kept, `−0.059`), ReLU² FFN
  (dropped), value-embedding skip (dropped).

### The decay-phase punchline

The §7.5 final run's val curve has three regimes:

1. **Warmup → fast initial drop (0 → ~5,000 steps).** Linear LR ramp 0 → `1.414e-3`
   over the first 100 steps, then val drops 4.34 → 3.76 by step 5,000.
2. **Stable-LR plateau (~10,000 → ~36,000 steps, the "S" of WSD).** Val
   bounces in 3.39–3.50 with ±0.04 nats noise. **Not** "the model is full" —
   it's an LR-schedule artifact; the same model drops another `~0.2` nats
   just by annealing the LR.
3. **Decay phase (36,000 → 45,000 steps, the "D" of WSD).** LR linearly
   decays `1.414e-3 → 0` over 9,000 steps. Val drops 3.397 → 3.190 — a
   sharp, monotonic improvement that **on its own contributes −0.207 nats**,
   bigger than the entire Tier-1 stack (−0.082 nats) combined.

This is the third time the same lesson appeared:

| Phase | Decay-tail gain | Number of decay steps |
|---|---:|---:|
| §7.2 cosine 10K (TinyStories) | −0.10 nats | 9,500 |
| §7.4 cosine 10K (OWT) | −0.44 nats | 9,500 |
| §7.5 WSD 45K (OWT leaderboard) | −0.21 nats | 9,000 |

Decay finds a sharper minimum than the stable-LR phase can settle into.
WSD just makes the decay window an explicit fraction of training rather
than an integral of a cosine curve.

### §7.5 generation observations

Five samples in [`experiments/generations/leaderboard/`](../generations/leaderboard/),
mirroring the §7.4 layout for direct apples-to-apples comparison.
The −0.83 nats / 2.29× PPL improvement is **clearly visible at the paragraph
level**:

- **Topic stability is much better.** §7.4's headline sample swerves
  ice → butterflies → "PCK" → tables → "the game" inside a single paragraph.
  §7.5's samples stay on a single coherent topic for multiple paragraphs
  (soccer-development press release; presidential-speech transcript).
- **Named-entity consistency is real.** §7.5 re-uses "USA Soccer Development
  Academy" as a recurring institution and attributes quotes to a consistent
  invented title. §7.4 invented one-off names that never reappeared.
- **Discourse structure shows up.** Press-release lead paragraph + quote
  attribution + logistics paragraph in expected order; presidential-speech
  sample uses standard press-corps formatting.
- **Failure modes are still there, just shorter-scope.** Low-temperature
  loops (`"really, really, really, ..."` at `temp=0.7`); the no-EOS sample
  invents `"Atlantic Atlantic birds"` and gets stuck repeating. Same kinds
  of small-LM bugs as §7.4, just at narrower scope.

The qualitative gap mirrors the loss gap: §7.5 operates at an effective
branching factor of ~24 (vs ~56 for §7.4), so each local choice is much
sharper and the model has roughly 2× more "budget" per token to keep the
discourse plan alive.

### A100 → H100 hardware substitution

The leaderboard rule specifies `1.5 h on H100 bf16-dense, no FP8`. We do not
have an H100 in this environment. We used the publicly-cited
**2.0–2.5× A100→H100 dense bf16 ratio** as the conversion factor and the
canonical **2.17× midpoint = 11,700 s on A100-40GB** as the budget. The
final run used 10,584 s, which converts to:

| Conversion ratio | H100-equivalent |
|---|---:|
| Conservative 2.0× | 5,292 s = **1.470 h** |
| Canonical 2.17× | 4,876 s = **1.354 h** |
| Optimistic 2.5× | 4,233 s = **1.176 h** |

All three land **inside the 1.5 h H100 envelope**. The wallclock-budget
guard (`--max-wallclock-sec 11400` flag in `scripts/train_lm.py`, with a
unit test in `tests/test_train_lm_script.py`) was present as a second-line
defense but never triggered — the run finished naturally when WSD's linear
decay reached LR=0 at step 45,000.

## Cross-cutting learnings

A handful of themes that compounded across §7.2–§7.5:

1. **Decay > warmup, three times in a row.** TinyStories (`−0.10` nats),
   OWT 10K (`−0.44` nats over the cosine tail), OWT leaderboard
   (`−0.21` nats over the WSD tail at 45K steps). Most of the win in any
   non-trivial schedule comes from late-stage low-LR refinement, not from
   the warmup. **WSD makes this explicit** by parameterizing the decay
   window as a fraction of training (`decay_frac=0.2`); cosine bundles the
   same effect into an integral.

2. **Architectural defaults stack.** RMSNorm, pre-norm, RoPE, SwiGLU — each
   §7.3 ablation removed one and showed a measurable cost (RMSNorm:
   instability + `+0.10` nats; post-norm: total failure to learn at our LR;
   RoPE: `+0.08` nats; SwiGLU: optimizer warmup that vanishes at convergence).
   §7.5 confirmed that the SwiGLU choice held *even at scale* — ReLU²
   regressed by `+0.020` nats at `(d=1024, L=8)`.

3. **Tier-1 quality wins stack super-linearly.** QK-Norm + tied embeddings
   + soft-cap + z-loss as singles summed to `−0.065` nats; stacked the same
   four hit `−0.082`. The mods compound because each one removes a
   *different* failure mode — QK-Norm bounds dot products, tying frees
   parameter budget, soft-cap bounds individual logit outliers, z-loss
   bounds the collective logsumexp. Visible only by stacking, not by
   summing singles.

4. **Speedrun mods don't always transfer.** The NanoGPT speedrun's two
   non-Muon Tier-2 favorites — ReLU² FFN and value-embedding skips —
   regressed or were neutral at our scale. The optimizer (Muon) and the
   schedule (WSD) and the longer context all transferred cleanly. The
   pattern: *infrastructure-level* and *optimization-level* speedrun mods
   transfer; *FFN-shape* and *skip-topology* mods are scale-sensitive.

5. **The sqrt-LR rule transfers across both bs and corpus, with caveats.**
   Anchor at `bs=128, lr=2e-3` on TinyStories. The rule's prediction at
   `bs=192` was right within `0.003` nats. On OWT at `bs=96` the optimum
   was in the same shallow basin. **For `d_model` scaling**, μP-style
   `lr / sqrt(d_new / d_old)` was a *seed* — at fixed-LR / 2000 steps the
   actual optimum was lower than the muP guess (lr=1.0e-3 won over
   1.414e-3); at the longer WSD-scheduled final run the optimum drifted
   *back up* to `1.414e-3`. The cheap 3-LR mini-sweep is what catches the
   discrepancy in either direction.

6. **Re-anchor LR after every loss-landscape change.** §7.5 did this four
   times (Phase 0.5: new infra; Phase 1.5: new arch with QK-Norm; Phase 2c:
   new model size; Phase 4.5: new mods stack with WSD + ctx=1024). Each
   re-anchor was 3 LRs × 1500–2000 steps ≈ 30 minutes; total LR-tuning
   overhead was ~3 h, paid back many times over by the 3-h final run not
   committing to a wrong LR.

7. **Throughput plateaus before optimization quality does.** GPU utilization
   saturates by `bs ≈ 32–64` (~63 k tok/s on A10G fp32, ~220 k tok/s on
   A100 bf16+SDPA+compile at our shape) but val loss keeps improving with
   batch size, scale, and total training tokens up to the memory ceiling.
   **Use the largest bs that fits, and let the LR schedule do the rest.**

8. **Memory-driven choices show up everywhere.** A10G `bs=128` OOM at
   `vocab=32k` (§7.4); A100-40GB `bs=192` OOM with the full Tier-1 stack at
   `(d=1024, L=8)` because the `(B, T, V)` fp32 logits gradient is 6 GiB
   alone (§7.5 Phase 2a); the `cross_entropy` rewrite that eliminated a 3 GiB
   fp32 cast (§7.5 Phase 1.5); `compile-mode=default` instead of
   `reduce-overhead` to skip CUDA-graph double-buffering. Memory is what
   sets the upper batch / context / model size, not throughput.

9. **The same loss number means very different things on different data.**
   TinyStories val `1.35` (PPL 3.85) is a fluent child-story generator;
   OWT val `4.02` (PPL 55.6) is a per-paragraph stylist that drifts
   topically; OWT val `3.19` (PPL 24.3) is a per-section topic-stable
   generator with consistent named entities. All three are "the same
   architecture family"; data and compute are what's different.

10. **Hardware substitution should be explicit and ratio-banded.** §7.5's
    A100 → H100 conversion uses three points of the public 2.0–2.5×
    bf16-dense ratio band (`1.176 h / 1.354 h / 1.470 h` H100-equivalent
    for the same 10,584 s A100 run). All three fit the 1.5 h envelope, so
    the budget claim is robust to which point of the band is chosen.
