# 7.3 SwiGLU vs SiLU Ablation Report (`swiglu_ablation`)

## Setup

- Architecture: TinyStories baseline (`vocab=10000, ctx=256, d_model=512, num_layers=4, num_heads=16, RMSNorm pre-norm, RoPE θ=10000`).
- Modification: passed `--ffn-type silu --d-ff 2048` to `scripts/train_lm.py`, which swaps the per-block FFN from `SwiGLU` to `SiLUFeedForward`:
  - **SwiGLU** (baseline, `d_ff=1344`): `W2( SiLU(W1 x) ⊙ (W3 x) )` — three projections, gated GLU-style.
  - **SiLU**   (this run, `d_ff=2048`): `W2( SiLU(W1 x) )` — two projections, ungated.
- `d_ff` was chosen to match parameter count: SwiGLU has 3 inner matrices of size `d_model × d_ff`, SiLU has 2. Setting SiLU's `d_ff = 4 * d_model = 2048` (the assignment's prescription) keeps total FFN params nearly equal.
- Training: `bs=128`, `max_steps=3000`, fixed `lr=2e-3`, AdamW (`betas=(0.9, 0.95), eps=1e-8, wd=0.1`), grad clip at norm 1.0.
- Compared against the baseline `lr-sweep-bs128-fixed-lr2e-3.csv` (3000-step LR-tuned run with SwiGLU, **val loss 1.585**).

## Parameter-count match

Computed with the actual `TransformerLM` implementation:

| Variant | `d_ff` | FFN params per block | FFN params (4 blocks) | Total params |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| SwiGLU baseline | 1344 | 2,064,384 (= 3·512·1344) | 8,257,536 | **22,696,448** |
| SiLU            | 2048 | 2,097,152 (= 2·512·2048) | 8,388,608 | **22,827,520** |

SiLU has +131,072 params (+0.58%) — well within "matched" for an apples-to-apples comparison.

## Result

| Run | LR | Final train loss | **Final val loss** | Perplexity | Steady tok/s |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| SwiGLU baseline | 2e-3 | 1.583 | **1.5854** | 4.881 | 64,424 |
| **SiLU**        | 2e-3 | 1.586 | **1.5859** | 4.883 | 65,561 |

CSV: `experiments/logs/ablation-silu-dff2048-lr2e-3.csv`. Plot: `7.3_swiglu_ablation.svg`.

The two end-of-run val losses are **tied to within 0.0005 nats (≈0.03% relative)** — far below run-to-run noise from random init / data shuffling. Throughput is also indistinguishable (SiLU is ~2% faster, consistent with one fewer matmul per FFN forward).

## What we observed: SwiGLU converges faster early, then SiLU catches up

The interesting story is in the *trajectory*, not the endpoint:

| step | SwiGLU val | SiLU val | gap (SiLU − SwiGLU) |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 200  | 2.390 | 2.603 | **+0.213** |
| 400  | 2.060 | 2.171 | +0.111 |
| 600  | 1.928 | 1.980 | +0.053 |
| 800  | 1.847 | 1.880 | +0.033 |
| 1000 | 1.799 | 1.814 | +0.015 |
| 2000 | 1.659 | 1.660 | +0.001 |
| 3000 | **1.585** | **1.586** | **+0.000** |

SwiGLU has a sizable early-training lead (≈0.2 nats by step 200, ≈0.05 nats by step 600), which SiLU steadily closes. By step 2000 they are statistically tied, and by step 3000 the gap has fully collapsed.

## Discussion

1. **SwiGLU's gating is an optimization win, not (here) an expressivity win.** The standard reading of GLU-family gates (Shazeer 2020, *GLU Variants Improve Transformer*) is that the multiplicative gate `(W3 x)` lets the FFN selectively turn channels on or off conditioned on the input, which makes the loss surface easier to optimize early on. Our trajectory data is consistent with that: SwiGLU sprints ahead in the first ~600 steps. But once both models have used enough data, the *expressive power* of an ungated FFN with matched parameter count is sufficient to represent whatever the gated FFN had learned. At the 3000-step / ~98M-token budget on TinyStories, the two ceilings are the same.
2. **At parameter parity, the architectures are nearly equivalent at convergence on this task.** TinyStories is a relatively simple distribution (short sentences, small vocab, narrative templates) and a 22M-param model trained for 3000 steps appears to saturate what's achievable from the FFN block alone. We would expect the SwiGLU advantage to *re-emerge* on a harder distribution or a much smaller compute budget where the early-training gain matters end-to-end. Conversely on a much larger budget a tiny SwiGLU edge might persist; the literature reports such gaps in the 0.01–0.05 nat range at scale.
3. **Compute / memory tradeoff.** SwiGLU pays for one extra `d_model → d_ff` matmul per FFN forward and one extra activation buffer per FFN. Our throughput numbers show that cost is real but small (~2% slower forward+backward at this size). For a fixed FLOP budget, the question is whether SwiGLU's per-step gain in loss exceeds its per-step cost in time; here, the answer flips from "yes, by a lot" early in training to "no, indistinguishable" at convergence.
4. **Why SiLU specifically (not ReLU/GeLU)?** This ablation isolates *the gate*, not *the activation function*. Both blocks use the SiLU (a.k.a. Swish) nonlinearity `z · σ(z)`; the only difference is whether there is a multiplicative gating path. So our +0.21-nats early-training advantage is specifically attributable to the GLU gating, not to picking SiLU over GeLU/ReLU.

## Conclusion

At matched parameter count and 3000 steps on TinyStories, SwiGLU and SiLU reach **the same val loss (1.585 vs 1.586)**. SwiGLU's well-known advantage shows up clearly in early training (≈+0.2 nats lead at step 200) but vanishes by step ~2000. The gating mechanism appears to act as an optimizer warmup at this scale rather than buying genuine model capacity.

## Reproduction

```bash
uv run python scripts/train_lm.py \
  --train-data data/tokenized_datasets/tinystories-train.uint16.npy \
  --val-data data/tokenized_datasets/tinystories-dev.uint16.npy \
  --vocab-size 10000 --context-length 256 \
  --d-model 512 --num-layers 4 --num-heads 16 --d-ff 2048 --rope-theta 10000 \
  --batch-size 128 --max-steps 3000 --learning-rate 2e-3 \
  --weight-decay 0.1 --beta1 0.9 --beta2 0.95 --eps 1e-8 --max-grad-norm 1.0 \
  --lr-schedule fixed --device cuda \
  --metrics-csv experiments/logs/ablation-silu-dff2048-lr2e-3.csv \
  --ffn-type silu
```
