## Fluency Commentary

The outputs are quite fluent for a 4-layer / `d_model=512` model trained on ~327M TinyStories tokens. They reproduce the dataset's distinctive style well: short declarative sentences, named protagonists ("Lily", "Tim", "Tom", "Sue"), simple dialogue with quoted speech, and a moral or wrap-up at the end. Grammar is mostly clean (subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, pronoun anaphora), and stories terminate naturally with `<|endoftext|>`, indicating the model has learned the document boundary.

There are still local incoherences typical of small-LM TinyStories generations: e.g. in the "Lily / fragile egg" story the butterfly "flew around her neck" then is "put back in the tree" and somehow that "keeps the fragile egg safe" - the entities get reused as the story progresses but the causal chain is loose. Similarly, the "wealthy man" and "grumpy cat" stories repeat the action "play the game" or "want to play" several times without much progression. This is the small-model equivalent of plausible-but-shallow narrative.

## Factors That Affect Output Quality

Two factors I observed directly, plus a few additional ones worth noting:

1. **Training compute / total tokens seen.** The reference example in the assignment was produced after a longer training budget; our model trained on ~327M tokens converged to validation loss ~1.47 (perplexity ~4.4). The headline reference example reads more fluidly than ours, especially in narrative arc and entity tracking. The "low-resource" 40M-token sample shown in the handout is visibly shakier than ours, consistent with this. More tokens / more steps closes the gap.
2. **Decoding hyperparameters (`temperature`, `top_p`).** Lower temperature (`0.7`) gave slightly more deterministic, "safer" stories with higher repetition of common patterns ("Lucy and Tim played together all day"). Higher temperature (`1.0`) introduced more variety but also more odd phrasings like "I am very good at it" attributed to a child playing catch. `temperature=0.8, top_p=0.95` was the best balance: enough randomness for variety, enough truncation to keep token choices on-distribution. With no nucleus truncation (`top_p=1.0`) or very high temperature, the model can drift into ungrammatical low-probability tails.
3. **Tokenizer / vocab quality.** A 10k BPE vocab trained on TinyStories captures common words and morphology cleanly, so the model rarely splits a real word into bizarre subwords. A poorly trained or mismatched tokenizer would break fluency immediately.
4. **Model capacity (depth, width, heads).** Our model is small (4 layers, `d_model=512`, ~50M params total). A deeper / wider model at the same training budget tends to track entities and longer-range dependencies more accurately, reducing the kind of mid-story entity drift we saw with the butterfly / egg.
5. **Architecture choices (RMSNorm, RoPE, SwiGLU).** Our 7.3 ablations confirm these choices each contribute meaningfully - removing any of them at the same training budget yields a measurably worse validation loss, which directly shows up as lower fluency at decode time.

## Reproduction Commands

```bash
uv run python scripts/generate_lm.py \
  --checkpoint-path experiments/checkpoints/tinystories-lr-tuned-fixed.pt \
  --vocab-path experiments/tinystories-bpe-10k-vocab.json \
  --merges-path experiments/tinystories-bpe-10k-merges.txt \
  --prompt "Once upon a time" \
  --max-new-tokens 320 --temperature 0.8 --top-p 0.95 \
  --eos-token "<|never|>" \
  --vocab-size 10000 --context-length 256 \
  --d-model 512 --num-layers 4 --num-heads 16 --d-ff 1344 --rope-theta 10000 \
  --device cuda
```

(Set `--eos-token "<|endoftext|>"` to stop at the first end-of-text token.)
